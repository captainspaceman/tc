While more goes into valuing a used vehicle that we can outline here, did you know that wear and tear usually falls into one of two categories?

Two Categories of Wear and Tear

Minor

Small surface dings, scratches or cracks. Tire wear consistent with the mileage. No mechanical or electrical issues.

Excessive

Large dings, scratches or cracks that would be expensive to repair. Major mechanical or electrical issues. Missing parts.

If you only have minor wear and tear, and everything else is in order, then you might be pleasantly surpirsed at the trade-in value you’ll get towards your purchase of a new vehicle.

Dear Mr. Farrell,

Now that you're shopping for a new vehicle, your existing used car or truck is officially a commodity that is rapidly losing its value.

Scary thought, right? Not to worry, we have a few tips to maximize the value of your used car, so you can get the most out of it when it's time to trade-up.

Three Things You Can Do Right Now

Get it detailed

A professional detailing service can have a car with minor wear and tear looking almost new. If you're the DIY type, you can tackle this task yourself if you've got the time.

Get it serviced

An oil change, a tire rotation plus all the little ins and outs of routine car maintenance can add up to a more valuable trade-in when it's time to turn in the keys.

Gather all the records

A clean service record—and the paper as proof of service to show to the dealer—gives the buyer of your used car or truck an extra confidence boost that the vehicle has held its value.

Taking these small steps to increase your trade-in value can go a long way in getting the most value twoards your purchase of a new car or truck.
